# Abstract

Pediatric patients navigating hospital environments require age-appropriate, institutionally grounded information that general-purpose AI systems are not designed to provide. We present an age-targeted fine-tuning framework for a virtual hospital guide deployed within a digital recreation of Victoria Hospital (London, Ontario). Using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on 4-bit quantized Llama 3.2 Instruct models (1B and 3B), we train separate adapters for children aged 5–11 and adolescents aged 12–18 on Apple Silicon (Mac Mini M4, 16 GB) — modeling deployment in resource-limited settings. Training data consists of ~475 synthetic examples per adapter generated by Claude Opus 4.6 from Victoria Hospital's public patient materials, following a LIMA-inspired curation approach. We evaluate Standard (r=8, 16 layers) and Fast (r=4, 8 layers) configurations across both model sizes and age groups (8 training runs total) on 50 held-out prompts per group. Fine-tuned adapters substantially outperform zero-shot base models on readability (72–84% of child-targeted responses meeting FK ≤ 7.0 vs. 12–14% baseline) and register separation (inter-role classifier accuracy 0.89–0.94 vs. 0.66–0.70). A crossover pattern suggests optimal adapter capacity may be model-size-dependent. Only the Fast-1B configuration meets the 1.0-second VR latency target (0.93s). All configurations pass a limited rule-based safety probe; this is not a comprehensive safety certification. These results demonstrate that parameter-efficient adaptation on consumer hardware is feasible for pediatric clinical NLP, while flagging single-seed variance and synthetic-only evaluation as primary open questions.
